Cross-team coordination for a team running two operations

Operations · Internal communications · Hospitality

Hospitality · hotel and F&B operation Lisbon, Portugal Nine-person team across two operations 3 years · reporting to the General Manager

Starting point

One team, two operations, and schedules printed on paper that staff were not consistently checking. Recurring cross-department conflict, in a high-volume service environment where a missed shift is visible to customers the same day.

Context

Hospitality. Riviera Center is a Portuguese hotel and food-and-beverage operation in a high-volume service environment. I was the sole people-operations generalist for a nine-person team shared across both operations, reporting directly to the General Manager. Scheduling, cross-department communication, supplier coordination, and seasonal onboarding were all mine to run.

Challenge

Two operations drawing on one shared team meant coordination was constant, and it was running on paper schedules that staff were not consistently consulting. The result was recurring cross-department conflict: shifts misread, coverage assumed rather than confirmed, the same failures repeating week to week. In a high-volume service environment a missed shift is not an administrative error. It is a service failure a customer sees the same day, and it cannot be recovered the next morning.

Approach

I replaced the paper schedules with a twice-weekly briefing structure, so the team confirmed coverage and changes face to face rather than reading a sheet nobody trusted. I built a documented supplier ordering system: a shared spreadsheet tracking stock levels and reorder points, replacing a verbal process that left no record. For peak season I designed a structured onboarding for four seasonal hires, aligned to the operational standards of the busiest period rather than a generic induction.

Why twice-weekly, not daily or weekly

A daily briefing burns time a nine-person service team does not have. A weekly one leaves too long for the schedule to drift before anyone catches it. Twice a week matched the rate the schedule actually changed, so the structure held without becoming a cost of its own.

Outcomes

  • Zero scheduling gaps and zero service disruptions across three years
  • Recurring cross-department conflicts eliminated once the briefing structure replaced the paper schedules
  • Zero supply gaps across the full three-year period after the ordering system went in
  • Full operational readiness on day one for all four seasonal hires

Why this transfers

The coordination that breaks across a hotel and an F&B floor is the same coordination that breaks when a Series A startup adds its second and third team: product, sales, and customer success. Paper schedules or Slack threads, the failure mode is identical: information people stop trusting, and cross-functional conflict that looks operational but starts in communication. The fix is a rhythm people actually use.
