Let's talk: workplace wellbeing campaign

People & Culture · Sensitive-topic communication · Trust-first copy architecture

Context

Gil Ferreira Ltda is a small private services organization in Portugal with a small operational team. I work as HR and People Operations Manager and have held the role for over seven years, reporting directly to senior leadership.

Challenge

The company wanted to run a wellbeing initiative, but faced a predictable barrier. Employees had learned to distrust institutional offers of help. Previous campaigns about mental health and stress management were ignored because the messaging felt like the company offering care on the company's terms, not the employee's. The real problem was not the absence of wellbeing support. It was the absence of trust that the company would not use vulnerability against the employee.

Approach

I designed a three-session workplace event positioned as a peer conversation rather than an HR initiative. The architecture was built on pre-empting the specific objections that stop employees from engaging.

The headline acknowledged the avoidance pattern directly: "Let's talk about what we usually don't." The subheading addressed the core suspicion: "On your terms, not the company's." The campaign emphasized that HR would not be in the room, that attendance would not flag records, and that listening without speaking was an acceptable participation mode. The format removed every signal of institutional control: no registration form, no stage, no audit trail.

The event ran during work hours with three concrete sessions covering workplace stress, peer experience, and open conversation. Deliverables included the event invite (digital and print), session agendas, facilitator briefing materials, and follow-up communications.

Outcomes

  • Designed and delivered a workplace wellbeing campaign at the company
  • Established a peer-led format as an alternative to standard HR-run wellbeing initiatives
  • Created a trust-first copy architecture template that I now apply to other resistant-audience internal campaigns
  • Reframed institutional communication for an employee audience that had previously disengaged from HR programming

Skills demonstrated

Internal communications, trust-first messaging, audience psychology, copy architecture, resistant-audience engagement, mental health literacy, workshop facilitation, employee advocacy.

Annotated decisions

Headline: "Let's talk about what we usually don't"

The second-person plural "we" positions the employee and the organization on the same side of the avoidance pattern. Most wellbeing messaging emphasizes what the company is offering. This headline names the shared problem first, so the employee feels understood before being asked for anything. It also names the avoidance pattern without shaming the audience for it. You reach a resistant audience by acknowledging that their skepticism is rational.

Subheadline: "On your terms, not the company's"

The biggest unstated objection in any corporate wellbeing communication is that the company benefits from employee openness more than the employee does. Addressing this directly is more persuasive than ignoring it. Many organizations assume they should downplay the company's interest in employee wellbeing. This copy does the opposite. It acknowledges the company's investment in knowing about employee struggles, then reassures the reader that this event is not that mechanism.

CTA: "Save your spot" instead of "Register now"

"Register now" creates a form and an audit trail. It feels like enrollment in a system. "Save your spot" is softer and implies the reader has already decided to attend. It reduces the friction between consideration and action by changing the psychological framing of the choice.

Reassurance: "HR is not in the room"

This is the objection that stops most employees from engaging with institutional wellbeing programs. It is rarely stated directly in company communications. Naming it directly, rather than burying it inside a general confidentiality statement, is what actually convinces someone to attend. Trust is earned by naming what people are afraid of. Pretending the fear does not exist does the opposite.
