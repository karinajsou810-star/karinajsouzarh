Cross-unit coordination protocol for hospitality

Operations · Service coordination · Conflict resolution

Context

A Portuguese hospitality company operating a hotel and an F&B restaurant that share a nine-person team. I worked there as HR and People Operations Coordinator from January 2016 through December 2018, reporting to the General Manager.

Challenge

The hotel and the F&B restaurant operated as separate units with separate schedules and separate staffing priorities, but they shared physical staff. This produced recurring conflicts about who would cover what during peaks. Scheduling decisions were made in silos, which caused service gaps and friction between departments. Seasonal hires arrived without integration into either unit's operational workflow, which created redundant training and inconsistent service standards.

Approach

I established standardized scheduling and communication workflows that replaced the previous siloed approach. I implemented twice-weekly scheduling briefings for all nine team members, using a single spreadsheet showing shift rotations, time-off assignments, and unit allocation across hotel and F&B. The briefings created shared context across both units before each operational week began. I created a structured onboarding process for four seasonal team members, aligning them with the operational standards of both units simultaneously rather than training them separately for each department.

I developed a documented protocol for handling shift preference conflicts: covering the negotiation structure, the compromise format, and the cross-departmental coverage obligations that attach to any approved preference change. The protocol converted what had previously been unstructured individual decisions into a repeatable format. When a staff member wanted to move from night to day shifts, the protocol produced a partial accommodation — two day shifts per week — with documented cross-departmental support obligations built into the shift agreement. Reception covering kitchen during high-volume periods was a condition of the shift change, written into the agreement from the start.

Outcomes

The scheduling system ran for three years with zero conflicts and zero service gaps. Four seasonal hires reached full operational readiness on day one, trained across both units simultaneously rather than per department. Shift preference disputes were resolved through the documented compromise protocol, with cross-departmental coverage obligations written into the agreements. No dispute required management escalation during the engagement.

Skills demonstrated

Scheduling and workflow coordination, cross-functional team management, conflict mediation, seasonal staff integration, service operations, staff communication, documentation, process design.
