About

What I do, and who I do it for.

Most HR generalists have a team. I have never had one, and that is why I work well for companies that cannot build one yet.

For eight years I have been the only HR person inside a privately held services company whose owners operate from the UK and are never on-site. I built every system from scratch: the onboarding toolkit, the scheduling protocol, the vendor evaluation framework, the governance model. None of them have needed structural changes in seven years. The principals have never needed to travel for routine HR governance.

My degree in Human Relations and Organizational Communication did not teach me those systems. What it taught me was how to see the gap between what an organization says it does and what it actually does. That gap is where every operational problem lives. I applied that framework inside a consulting engagement with an executive search firm: extracted the firm’s tacit recruitment methodology from the Director’s head, mapped it into a full process model, and delivered three versions of a retention framework: one for operations, one for new recruiter training, and one for client pitches.

I have also recruited at scale. At a national employment agency processing thousands of candidates per week, I screened 25 to 30 profiles per day and contributed to 15 placements per week across three protected candidate groups, with zero eligibility mismatches. That throughput requires a system, not a habit. The system comes from knowing what information you actually need versus what a form asks for. The academic training is where that distinction came from.

Methodology

Three diagnostic tools I use to figure out what is actually broken before I design a fix.

5-system communication diagnostic
Maps internal communication across five operational systems to find where a customer-facing breakdown actually starts. Used when the problem looks like a service issue but the root cause is internal.

Structured knowledge extraction
A diagnostic interview method for organizations with strong intuitive practice and no documented model. Surfaces the rules operators follow but cannot articulate. Result: a documented operational model that matches what the organization actually does.

Baseline measurement survey
A quantitative instrument deployed before designing any intervention. Draws on diagnostic work across hospitality, food distribution, and private services to show where communication quality correlates with disengagement. Gives you a concrete number before you spend on a fix.

All three share one operating principle: what an organization actually does is often different from what it describes doing. Specificity is what makes a framework useful.
