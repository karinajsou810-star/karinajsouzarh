Let's talk: workplace wellbeing campaign

People & Culture · Sensitive-topic communication · Trust-first design

Context

A private services company in Portugal, with three permanent staff and ownership permanently based in the UK. No on-site management. I am the sole HR function — no HR department, no comms team, no external agency support — and have been for eight years, reporting directly to senior leadership in another country.

Challenge

The company wanted to run a wellbeing initiative. The barrier was not resources. It was trust.

Previous campaigns on mental health and workplace stress had been ignored. Employees had learned that institutional offers of help tend to arrive on the company's terms, not theirs. The messaging felt managed. The subtext employees read was: the company wants to know about your struggles. That reading was rational, and it killed engagement before the first session.

The real problem was not the absence of wellbeing support. It was the absence of any reason for employees to believe that showing vulnerability would not be used against them.

There was no team to hand this to. No external comms support. No budget for an agency. I had to design the campaign, write the copy, and build the format myself. The solution had to be one that a single HR practitioner could execute without institutional infrastructure, and one that could work on an audience that had already decided not to trust it.

Approach

I designed a three-session workplace event positioned as a peer conversation, not an HR initiative.

The architecture was built around pre-empting the specific objections that stop employees from engaging with anything labelled wellbeing. Every design decision was made to remove signals of institutional control before the audience had a chance to trigger them.

The headline named the avoidance pattern directly. The subheading addressed the core suspicion about who the event was actually for. The copy stated explicitly that HR would not be in the room, that attendance would not be flagged on any record, and that listening without speaking was a valid participation mode. There was no registration form, no stage, no audit trail.

The three sessions covered workplace stress, peer experience, and open conversation. Deliverables included the event invite in digital and print formats, session agendas, facilitator briefing materials, and follow-up communications. I wrote and produced all of them.

Outcomes

Employees who had not engaged with any previous HR-run wellbeing programming attended all three sessions. The format worked because it removed the conditions that had caused disengagement in earlier campaigns.

The peer-led, HR-absent structure became the operating template for subsequent resistant-audience internal communications at the company. Any internal campaign where employee trust in the institution is the primary barrier now uses the same architectural logic: name the suspicion, remove the audit trail, and reframe who the communication is actually for.

The copy architecture developed for this campaign is documented and reusable. It has been applied to other internal communications where the audience has a rational reason to disengage from HR-originated messaging.

Skills demonstrated

This case study is not primarily about internal communications. It is about what happens when the only HR person in a company needs to build trust before anything else will work. Building a governance model or an onboarding toolkit is straightforward when people believe you are there for them. Getting that belief, when the institution has already lost it, requires a different kind of HR work: diagnostic thinking about what employees are actually afraid of, and the ability to translate that diagnosis into a format and a structure that removes the fear before it can block engagement. That is the work this case study documents.

Annotated decisions

Headline: "Let's talk about what we usually don't"

The diagnostic question before writing a word was: why did previous wellbeing initiatives fail? The answer was not that employees lacked problems. It was that the framing of every previous initiative positioned the company as the problem-solver, which meant employees had to admit a problem to an institution that might use that admission against them. The headline is designed to sit on the same side of that problem as the employee. It names the shared avoidance before asking for anything. An HR practitioner who has correctly diagnosed the trust deficit does not lead with what the company is offering. They lead with what the employee already knows.

Subheadline: "On your terms, not the company's"

Employees in most organizations have a rational read on corporate wellbeing programs: the company benefits from knowing about their struggles. That read is not paranoia. It is accurate. The employee relations decision here was not to pretend the conflict of interest does not exist, but to name it and draw a line around it. The subheadline is an HR boundary made visible in the communication: it tells the employee where the company's interest ends. Organizations that skip this step lose the audience before the first session. The ones that name it build the trust that makes engagement possible.

CTA: "Save your spot" instead of "Register now"

The CTA decision followed from a structural HR decision: no registration, no audit trail. Attendance would not be logged. If employees could not be identified as participants, they could not be flagged, evaluated, or managed based on what they said in the room. The language change from "Register now" to "Save your spot" is secondary. The primary decision was to remove the data collection mechanism entirely. Communication that promises confidentiality while still capturing names is not confidential. The HR structural decision came first; the copy followed from it.

Reassurance: "HR is not in the room"

This was an organizational design decision before it was a communication decision. HR was structurally removed from the sessions: not just absent in practice, but absent by design and stated explicitly in the materials. That decision came from diagnosing the specific mechanism that had caused previous programs to fail. Employees did not trust that what they said in a company-sponsored wellbeing session would stay there. The reassurance in the communication works because the structural decision had already been made. A written assurance with no structural backing is not a reassurance. It is a liability.
