Talent lifecycle and retention framework audit

Talent strategy · Consulting engagement · Retention framework design

Context

Solução Futuro is an executive search firm in Leiria, Portugal, specializing in qualified technician and middle-management recruitment, with operations across Portugal and an expansion to Angola. I led a four-month talent strategy consulting engagement focused on attraction and retention, applying structured field research to document and codify the firm's full-cycle talent management model.

Challenge

Executive search firms typically measure success by placement fee revenue rather than by candidate retention. Solução Futuro had a strong intuitive model for candidate and client retention, built over time by its Executive Director, but no formal documentation of the framework or the operational accountability that distinguished it from a standard recruitment-fee firm. The company described retention without defining what it actually did post-placement. My brief was to extract the implicit framework into a documented operational model that the firm could communicate to clients and teach to new staff.

Approach

I applied a structured stakeholder engagement methodology, conducting a fifteen-question diagnostic interview with the Executive Director, designed to surface both stated and unstated practices. Rather than survey what the firm thought it did, I observed and documented what it actually did. I mapped the full process cycle from client intake through candidate placement within a documented fifteen to twenty-five day operational window across three phases: diagnosis, conception and implementation, and consulting and integration support.

I documented the firm's sourcing strategy across three active recruitment channels: ATS database, LinkedIn executive search, and professional referral networks. I mapped selection criteria across ten professional recruitment categories (engineering, administrative, technical assistance, education, health, cleaning, hospitality, marketing, logistics, carpentry and welding) and documented how channel choice was determined by role seniority and technical profile requirements.

The most significant work was extracting the retention framework as a four-touchpoint accountability model: pre-placement (candidate selection, mentoring, and a parecer recommendation to the client), placement-day (physical accompaniment and integration support), quarterly follow-up (structured contact with both candidate and client), and redeployment (separated candidates retained in the placement pool for future opportunities). The framework revealed retention as ongoing accountability that runs throughout the candidate lifecycle, with quarterly check-ins built into staff workflows rather than treated as a discretionary post-placement service.

Outcomes

  • A fifteen to twenty-five day operational window documented across three distinct phases, with deliverables mapped to each
  • A ten-category sourcing strategy documented with explicit channel selection logic by role profile
  • A four-touchpoint retention framework extracted and documented as operationally distinct from placement-only competitors
  • Empirical field research methodology applied to talent strategy work, producing documentation usable for client communication and staff training
  • An operational model that articulated the firm's actual differentiator: not who they place, but how long they stay involved

Skills demonstrated

Stakeholder interview methodology, process mapping, operational analysis, talent strategy documentation, recruitment channel assessment, retention framework design, field research, qualitative data analysis.

Annotated decisions

A fifteen-question structured interview instead of a survey

Executive search practices are largely tacit. An owner knows what they do, but often cannot articulate the rules that guide their decisions. A survey would return "we prioritize candidate fit" without explaining what fit means or how it determines channel choice. A structured interview uses probing questions to surface unstated criteria. It also creates a conversation where the Executive Director can explain decisions in context rather than answering isolated questions. Interviews surface logic. Surveys surface opinion.

Field research over self-reported data

When you ask a recruitment firm "how do you source candidates?" they describe their ideal process. When you observe their actual sourcing decisions, you discover the heuristics that really drive channel choice. Solução Futuro described its sourcing as systematic, but observation showed it was rule-based and flexible: certain roles always went to ATS, certain roles required LinkedIn, certain roles came through referral networks for reasons the firm had never explicitly documented. Documentation without observation produces an aspirational model. Documentation with observation produces an accurate one.

Retention documented as four distinct touchpoints, not as a generic follow-up program

The difference between "we follow up with candidates" and "we conduct quarterly check-ins with candidate and client separately" is specificity. The first is a general commitment. The second is operationally useful. It tells staff when to reach out, who to contact, and what to accomplish on each call. It also reveals the actual differentiator. Most search firms place a candidate and consider the job done. Solução Futuro places a candidate and stays involved through quarterly accountability. Without specificity, a framework stays aspirational. Specificity is what moves it into daily operations.
