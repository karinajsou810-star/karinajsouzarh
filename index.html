About

What I do, and who I do it for.

Most HR people join a team. I never had one. That is why I work well for founders who cannot build one yet.

For eight years I ran the entire HR and people operations function solo — three permanent staff, seven external providers, owners based in the UK who were never on-site. Every system was mine to design from nothing: onboarding, scheduling, vendor evaluation, governance. None of it needed structural changes in seven years.

My degree in Human Relations and Organizational Communication trained me to do something specific: find the gap between how an organization thinks it works and how it actually works. That gap is where every people problem starts. I was reading it in the field before I had a name for it. The degree gave me the frameworks to apply it deliberately. Inside a consulting engagement with an executive search firm, I used that approach to extract the founder’s tacit recruitment methodology, map it into a documented process model, and deliver it in three versions: one for the team, one for training new recruiters, and one for client pitches.

I have also applied it at scale. Inside a government employment agency processing thousands of candidates per week across 53 regional offices, I ran full-cycle hiring across three different candidate profiles at once: 25 to 30 screened per day, ten placed per week as an individual contributor, no placement errors across the full engagement.

Methodology

Three diagnostic tools I use to figure out what is actually broken before I design a fix.

5-system communication diagnostic
Maps internal communication across five operational systems to find where a customer-facing breakdown actually starts. Used when the problem looks like a service issue but the root cause is internal.

Structured knowledge extraction
A diagnostic interview method for organizations with strong intuitive practice and no documented model. Surfaces the rules operators follow but cannot articulate. Result: a documented operational model that matches what the organization actually does.

Baseline measurement survey
A quantitative instrument deployed before designing any intervention. Draws on diagnostic work across hospitality, food distribution, and private services to show where communication quality correlates with disengagement. Gives you a concrete number before you spend on a fix.

All three share one operating principle: what an organization actually does is often different from what it describes doing. Specificity is what makes a framework useful.
